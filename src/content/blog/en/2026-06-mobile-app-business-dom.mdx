A mobile app is appealing because it puts your logo on your customers' home screen. But an app is not a website with an icon: it is a product in its own right, with its build cost, its updates and years of upkeep. Before you invest, ask one simple question: will your customers actually open it once a week?

## When an app truly makes sense

An app earns its place when usage is recurring. If your customers come back often — repeat orders from a restaurant, bookings at a salon, tracking a loyalty program — the home-screen icon becomes a useful shortcut rather than a gadget.

Three capabilities justify the cost of an app:

- **Loyalty and repeat orders.** A digital loyalty card, a saved cart, an order history: concrete reasons to come back.
- **Push notifications.** They surface an offer or a delivery at the right moment, where an email ends up ignored. It is the only channel that reaches the customer without them coming to find you.
- **Offline use.** In the West Indies, mobile coverage is uneven. An app can show a menu, a catalog or a loyalty card even with no signal, then sync when the connection returns.

If two of these three points describe your business, the app deserves a serious look.

## When a mobile site is enough (and costs less)

For many businesses, the main goal is to be found on Google and to present a clear offer. In that case a fast mobile site beats an app, for three reasons.

There is nothing to download: a customer finds your address, your hours or your menu in two clicks from a search. It works on every phone without depending on the App Store or the Play Store. And it updates in one place, with no app-store review.

A site also ranks on Google, which an app does not. If half your customers discover you by searching "restaurant + your town," an app cuts you off from that visibility.

## How much it costs, how long it takes

Let us be concrete about orders of magnitude. A well-built mobile brochure site is a matter of weeks of work. A native or hybrid app, with a backend and a release on both stores, is a matter of months.

The upfront cost is only part of the story. An app lives in a moving ecosystem: every year iOS and Android change their rules, and an unmaintained app eventually gets rejected from the stores or stops working. Plan for a yearly upkeep budget, not just a launch budget.

A site needs maintenance too, but the workload gap is real: one environment to watch, versus two platforms with separate requirements.

## How to decide in three questions

Before you choose, answer honestly:

1. **Would my customers come back at least once a week?** If usage is occasional, an app will sit forgotten in a folder.
2. **Do I need notifications, offline access or loyalty?** If none of these is essential, a site covers the need.
3. **How do my customers find me today?** If they come through a Google search, start with a solid site.

The takeaway: an app is an excellent retention tool, not an acquisition tool. If your first goal is visibility and converting visitors, invest in a fast mobile site. Keep the app for the day you have a base of loyal customers you want to talk to often.
