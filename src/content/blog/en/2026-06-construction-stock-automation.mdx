A construction tradesperson spends the day on site, not in front of a spreadsheet. Yet it is often a missing material — spotted too late — that costs a whole crew half a day. We helped a tradesperson facing exactly that problem: stock kept in his head and in a notebook, shortages discovered on the morning of a pour, orders placed in a rush. Here is how we put an AI agent on the job, and what it changed day to day.

## The problem: stock invisible until it runs out

Tracking used to rely on memory and a few scattered notes. As long as everything went well, it held. The day a bag of mortar, a type of screw or a length of conduit ran short, it was a scramble: call the supplier, wait for availability, sometimes send someone to buy at full price in store. The real cost was not the material — it was the lost time and the delayed jobs.

The need was clear: never again discover a shortage on the very day the material is needed. Not a heavy management tool to fill in every evening — he would never have filled it in — but something that works in the background and warns him at the right moment.

## Our approach: an agent, not one more piece of software

An AI agent is not a tool you open and feed by hand. It is an assistant you hand a task to, that watches information and acts on rules decided with you. We built it around four steps.

**Track stock without painful data entry.** The starting point of any automation is reliable data. Rather than an inventory to re-enter every day, we set up a light update: stock in on delivery, stock out per job, all in a few simple taps from the phone. The agent keeps the count up to date from there.

**Catch low levels before it is too late.** For each material that matters, we set a threshold together that accounts for the supplier's lead time. The agent watches continuously and triggers when a level nears the critical point — not when the shortage is already there.

**Prepare the order on its own.** When a threshold is reached, the agent drafts the purchase order: the right supplier, the right references, quantities calculated to restart from a healthy base. It generates a clean document, ready to go, and presents it to the tradesperson.

**Send it and keep the record.** After approval, the order goes to the supplier by email, and the agent centralizes the exchanges: what was ordered, when, from whom, and where it stands. No more purchase order lost at the bottom of an inbox.

## The human stays in control

One point we never compromise on: the agent prepares, the tradesperson decides. Nothing goes to a supplier without approval. That is deliberate. An order commits money and a deadline; that kind of decision stays human.

So the goal is not to take away control, but to take away the mental load. The tradesperson no longer spends his evenings wondering whether he has enough of a given product left for the week. The agent thinks about it for him and hands him an order that is already prepared: all that is left is to glance at it and confirm.

## What it changes in practice

The benefit is not measured in a technological gadget, it is measured in peace of mind. The surprise shortage, the one that freezes a job site, becomes rare because it is anticipated. The order, which used to take time and attention at the end of the day, shrinks to a quick review. And the history, long scattered, becomes readable at a glance.

In all honesty, we will not quote a percentage of time saved here: that would be a number invented to look good. What we can say is that the logic has changed. We moved from reactive management — putting out fires — to anticipatory management, without asking the tradesperson to become an administrator.

## Why it works for a tradesperson

Many management tools fail for one simple reason: they ask too much data entry from someone who has no time. An agent flips the load. It does the repetitive work — watching, calculating, drafting — and only calls on the human for what really matters: deciding.

The takeaway: if a task comes back every week, follows clear rules and is costly to forget, it is an excellent candidate for an agent. A construction tradesperson's stock management ticks all three boxes.
